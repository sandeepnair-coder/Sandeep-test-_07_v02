Ogilvy India Formula · Bewakoof · Fashion & Apparel
AI Campaign Strategy
Applies the Piyush Pandey–Ogilvy India method — ONE product truth, a two-level slogan, 3-act story plots, and campaign themes built to run for 10+ years.
The Slogan
"Bewakoof Raho."
"Stay Foolish. Stay Free."
The word 'Bewakoof' means foolish — but owning it as a badge of honour flips the script. The person who ignores social pressure to dress boldly, take risks, and express themselves is the freest person in the room. This is not a tagline. It is a philosophy that your core customer already lives by. You just need to name it for them.
Product Truth
Affordable, bold design that lets you dress like yourself — not like everyone else.
Human Emotion
The quiet courage of not caring what others think — and the joy that follows.
The Three-Act Formula

The product is never the hero. The situation is the hero. The product is the punchline.

ActDurationPurpose & ApproachBrain Chemistry
010–7sSet up a recognisable social situation. No brand. No product. Just a truth that makes the viewer say "that's me."Pattern recognition · Dopamine trigger
028–22sEscalate the tension. The character faces a choice between conformity and self-expression. Stakes feel real.Cortisol + empathy · Leaning forward
0323–30sCharacter chooses freedom. The Bewakoof piece appears naturally — worn, used, chosen. "Bewakoof Raho." fades in. Relief + aspiration.Oxytocin + brand encoding
Campaign Themes

01
The Brave Bewakoof
When being "different" turns out to be the point
Mines the tension between conformity and self-expression that every young Indian feels. The hero looks foolish by conventional standards — but turns out to be the only truly free person in the room. References Cadbury's girl-dancing-on-cricket-field insight: the most authentic person is always the most joyful. Works for campus, workplace, family occasions.
02
Bewakoof Bonds
The friends who got each other when no one else did
Celebrates the deep, irrational loyalty of friendships forged through shared weirdness. The most lasting Indian friendships aren't built on similarity — they're built on mutual bewakoofi. Emotionally adjacent to Fevicol's "bond that won't break" but rooted in Gen Z's found-family culture. Highest shareability of all four themes.
03
The Nationwide Bewakoof Map
Every city has its own beautiful madness
Celebrates regional quirks as authentic self-expression. What makes a Mumbaikar, Delhiite, or Bangalorean "bewakoof" in the best sense? Each city's version of joyful nonconformity becomes a campaign story. Creates strong regional relevance while building national identity — ideal for the Tier 2 expansion opportunity identified in Market Capture.

First Time Bewakoof
The terrifying, glorious first time you said "forget it, I'm doing this"
Life's best moments often look stupid to observers. First time wearing something bold, first time quitting a stable job, first time telling someone you love them in public. Mines the universal moment of choosing aliveness over safety. Aspirational without being unrelatable — every viewer has their own version of this story.
Story Plots

Filmable three-act plots. Product appears only in Act 3 — maximum 3–5 seconds of brand visibility.

Theme 01: The Brave Bewakoof
The Interview
Act 1 · 0–7s
A packed Mumbai local. Everyone in identical formal whites and navies. One person in a bold Bewakoof graphic tee stands out — heads turn, eyebrows raise. They don't notice. They're listening to music.
Act 2 · 8–22s
Interview waiting room. Same formal crowd. The interviewer — in her 40s, clearly seen it all — walks out and scans the room. Pauses at the bold tee. Long beat. The candidate's hands grip their portfolio tighter.
Act 3 · 23–30s
The interviewer smiles. "You're the one who sent the brief that actually said something." They walk in together. The Bewakoof logo is visible on the back of the tee for 3 seconds. "Bewakoof Raho." Cut to black.
Theme 02: Bewakoof Bonds
The 2AM Call
Act 1 · 0–7s
WhatsApp group "College Bewakoofe " lights up at 2am. Four faces illuminated by phones in four cities. "Raj's wedding is in 23 hours. Where is everyone?"
Act 2 · 8–22s
A montage of improbable journeys — a last-minute flight, a sleeper bus, a borrowed scooter at midnight. Each friend in their own Bewakoof piece, each grinning at the absurdity of what they're doing.
Act 3 · 23–30s
All four arrive simultaneously at the venue, dishevelled, laughing. Raj stares. "You're all idiots." Beat. "I knew you'd come." Group hug. Bewakoof logo visible across four different tees. "Bewakoof Raho."
Theme 04: First Time Bewakoof
The Quit
Act 1 · 0–7s
A grey open-plan office. Everyone in identical formals. One person staring at a screen showing their real passion — illustration, music, design. Their desk has a resignation letter in the drawer. It's been there 6 months.
Act 2 · 8–22s
They're about to close the drawer again. Then they see a colleague walk past — comfortable, authentic, unbothered. Something shifts. They open the letter. Type one final thing. Hit send.
Act 3 · 23–30s
Walking out of the building, jacket over shoulder, slow grin spreading. They call someone: "I did it." Beat. "Yes, I wore the Bewakoof shirt." Logo visible on their chest for 3 seconds. "Bewakoof Raho." Cut to black.
Common Pitfalls to Avoid
Making the product the hero in Act 1. The viewer must be hooked by the human situation before the brand appears. Product-first = skip.
Celebrity over character. Ogilvy's formula works because the audience sees themselves, not a star. Celebrity cameos dilute identification — use them only as supporting cast.
Price messaging in brand films. The moment you mention "affordable" in a brand film, you've broken the spell. Price belongs in performance ads, not in brand storytelling.
Changing the slogan every season. "Bewakoof Raho" must run for 5+ years across every touchpoint. Consistency is what turns a line into a cultural truth.
Urban-English monoculture. The brand's biggest opportunity is Tier 2 India. Every story plot should work in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu and Bengali — or spawn regional variants that do.
Overloading Act 3 with features. One product. One logo. One line. Maximum 5 seconds. Any longer and the emotional build of Acts 1–2 is wasted.
